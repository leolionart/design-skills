# Inclusive Accessibility First

## Thesis
Make readability, control clarity, and broad usability part of the visual identity, not an afterthought hidden behind generic product polish.

## Best when
- the brief says accessible, readable, inclusive, confidence through clarity, or public-service quality
- healthcare, education, enterprise, civic, nonprofit, or broad-audience products
- the interface includes forms, dense information, or sensitive tasks
- motion tolerance should stay low

## Distinguishing signals
- higher contrast discipline than `clean-saas-gradient`
- less tile density than `bento-grid` when density increases cognitive load
- bigger emphasis on readable controls, chunking, and state clarity
- modern but visibly dependable, not trend-led

## Layout directives
- chunk information into predictable groups
- keep the reading path obvious on first scan
- favor linear clarity over ambitious modular density
- use repetition only when it lowers cognitive effort

## Typography and surface directives
- use highly readable type sizes and line heights
- make labels, helper text, and states unambiguous
- keep surfaces plain enough to protect contrast
- use borders and spacing to clarify roles, not just to decorate

## Component behavior
- CTAs and controls should look immediately actionable
- forms need obvious grouping and error space
- cards should separate information, not create novelty
- tables, filters, and status chips should prioritize legibility

## Avoid
- startup gloss that weakens contrast
- bento density that makes scanning harder
- hover-only meaning
- low-contrast secondary text
- decorative motion that competes with comprehension

## Review and conversion cues
- If the page looks polished but not obviously readable, it is drifting toward `clean-saas-gradient`.
- If module density starts to dominate, it is drifting toward `bento-grid`.
- To convert from `clean-saas-gradient`, enlarge the clarity of type, controls, and grouping before changing style details.
