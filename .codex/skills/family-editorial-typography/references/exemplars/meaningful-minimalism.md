# Meaningful Minimalism

## Thesis
Use calm restraint, large breathing room, and very selective emphasis to make the interface feel serene, mature, and quietly premium rather than overtly luxurious.

## Best when
- the brief says calm, zen, serene, boutique, mature, or understated
- wellness, architecture, premium service, or founder-brand work
- the page should feel slow and intentional
- motion tolerance is low to medium

## Distinguishing signals
- quieter than `editorial-minimalism`
- warmer and more humane than `premium-monochrome`
- less typographic spectacle than `typography-first`
- relies on stillness, crop, and spacing more than drama

## Layout directives
- use fewer emphasis moments per page
- prefer calm, evenly paced sections over aggressive editorial contrast
- let whitespace create hierarchy before adding containers
- keep hero composition simple, open, and lightly framed

## Typography and surface directives
- use refined but gentle type contrast
- let headlines feel composed, not theatrical
- keep surfaces light, almost absent, or softly framed
- favor soft monochrome or natural tones over stark black-white luxury contrast

## Component behavior
- CTAs should be clear but low-pressure
- cards should be sparse and lightly separated
- proof blocks should feel integrated, not sales-heavy
- imagery should feel still, cropped, and intentional

## Avoid
- luxury-fashion coldness that pushes the page into `premium-monochrome`
- oversized expressive display that turns it into `typography-first`
- too many boxed sections
- launch-energy asymmetry that breaks the calm cadence

## Review and conversion cues
- If the page feels expensive but not calm, it is drifting toward `premium-monochrome`.
- If typography becomes the hero, it is drifting toward `typography-first`.
- To convert from `editorial-minimalism`, reduce contrast in pacing and remove excess editorial flourish.
