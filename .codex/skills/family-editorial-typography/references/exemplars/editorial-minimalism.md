# Editorial Minimalism

## Thesis
Use disciplined type, sharp spacing rhythm, and refined restraint to make the interface feel like a composed editorial artifact rather than a generic premium page.

## Best when
- the brief says editorial, manifesto, refined, Swiss-inspired, or reading-first
- luxury brands, agencies, portfolios, or commerce pages need composure
- the page should feel quiet but still visibly structured
- motion tolerance is low

## Distinguishing signals
- sharper and more publication-like than `meaningful-minimalism`
- warmer and less neutral than `premium-monochrome`
- less headline-dominant than `typography-first`
- relies on spacing rhythm and fine structure more than spectacle

## Layout directives
- use strong margins and calm section cadence
- let layout feel ordered, with one clear reading spine per section
- use containers and borders sparingly but deliberately
- keep hero composition open, restrained, and text-led

## Typography and surface directives
- use elegant type contrast with highly readable body rhythm
- let serif-sans pairing add tone without becoming ornamental
- favor hairline borders, light framing, and low decorative noise
- keep color restrained so composition does the work

## Component behavior
- CTA should stay clear but quiet
- supporting modules should frame content, not dominate it
- proof should feel curated and editorial, not sales-heavy
- imagery should reinforce mood and composition rather than steal focus

## Avoid
- loud accents that overpower the typography
- too many boxed sections or pills
- luxury cues without spacing discipline
- oversized expressive display that turns the page into `typography-first`

## Review and conversion cues
- If the page feels serene but not structurally editorial, it is drifting toward `meaningful-minimalism`.
- If the page feels luxury-neutral and colder, it is drifting toward `premium-monochrome`.
- To convert from louder marketing pages, remove decorative noise before refining type and spacing.
