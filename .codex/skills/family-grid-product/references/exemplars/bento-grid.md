# Bento Grid

## Thesis
Use varied tile sizes, modular grouping, and scan-friendly density to show many product benefits quickly without flattening everything into equal cards.

## Best when
- the brief says bento, modular, tile-based, feature-heavy, or overview-first
- SaaS, AI tools, and product marketing need many value points above the fold
- scanning speed matters as much as polish
- motion tolerance is low to medium

## Distinguishing signals
- denser and more tile-led than `clean-saas-gradient`
- more modular and higher-contrast in grouping than `inclusive-accessibility-first`
- uses tile scale and composition as the main storytelling device
- can feel more product-demo-like than a traditional landing page

## Layout directives
- vary tile sizes so importance is visible on first scan
- group related benefits into clear modular clusters
- keep one obvious reading order even when density increases
- let hero and proof sections connect into the same modular grammar

## Typography and surface directives
- keep headings concise and easy to scan
- use surfaces and borders to separate roles, not to decorate every tile equally
- allow more visible containers than in cleaner SaaS directions
- use color and accent sparingly so module structure remains primary

## Component behavior
- feature cards should differ by role, not only by copy
- proof, metrics, and CTA modules should be visible early
- dashboards and product shots can live inside larger anchor tiles
- filters or labels should help orientation, not add clutter

## Avoid
- equal cards everywhere
- density with no reading path
- decorative gradients hiding weak grouping
- over-polished surfaces that erase the modular logic

## Review and conversion cues
- If the page feels cleaner and less tile-driven, it is drifting toward `clean-saas-gradient`.
- If density starts to hurt readability and confidence, it is drifting toward `inclusive-accessibility-first` territory.
- To convert from linear product pages, introduce tile hierarchy before adding more content.
